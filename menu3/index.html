Angular Momentum Conservation:Spinning and Rolling

Items from Lab Kit Needed:

Piece of PVC Tube (Hollow Cylinder)
Piece of Wooden Dowel (Solid Cylinder)
Brightly Colored Tape
Ruler

In this lab, you will study some aspects of the motion shown the video above. In particular, we are interested in whether angular momentum and rotational kinetic energy are conserved through the transition from spinning and rolling to just rolling.

Theory

Fig.1 - From Jackson et al., Am. J. Phys., Feb. 2019

When a cylindrical object is spun as shown in the video, it actually also rolls without slipping on one edge. The figure above depicts a side view of this situation. Its motion has a lot of similarities to the precessing top discussed in lecture so please bear that in mind.

The important parameters are:

  • R R R: radius of cylinder

  • ℓ \ell ℓ: half of the length of the cylinder

  • Ω \Omega Ω: rate of rotation (angular velocity) about zzz-axis.

  • ψ˙ \dot{\psi} ψ˙​: rate of rotation about the cylinder's main principal axis.

  • e^z \hat{e}_z e^z​: Unit vector in the zzz direction. Same as k^\hat{k}k^.

  • e^1 \hat{e}_1 e^1​: Unit vector in the plane of the cylinder's perpendicular principal axis.

  • e^3 \hat{e}_3 e^3​: Unit vector in the direction of the cylinder's main principal axis (along its length).

  • rϕ r_\phi rϕ​: The part of the cylinder half-length ℓ\ellℓ that is parallel to the ground.

  • θ \theta θ: The angle that the cylinder makes with the vertical (measures how tipped over it is).

Since the edge of the cylinder traces out a circle of radius rϕr_\phirϕ​ on the ground while rolling about about its axis, we get

rϕΩ=Rψ˙ r_\phi\Omega = R\dot{\psi} rϕ​Ω=Rψ˙​

Now, we need to determine rϕ r_\phi rϕ​. Look at the diagram and try to determine what rϕ r_\phi rϕ​ would be in terms of R,ℓ R,\ell R,ℓ, and θ \theta θ.

rϕ=ℓsin⁡θ−Rcos⁡θ r_\phi = \ell\sin\theta - R\cos\theta rϕ​=ℓsinθ−Rcosθ

We can plug this into the first equation to find

ψ˙=(ℓRsin⁡θ−cos⁡θ)Ω \dot{\psi} = \left(\frac{\ell}{R}\sin\theta - \cos\theta\right)\Omega ψ˙​=(Rℓ​sinθ−cosθ)Ω

Next, we should find the angular velocity. From the diagram we see that

ω⃗=Ωe^z+ψ˙e^3 \vec{\omega} = \Omega\hat{e}_z + \dot{\psi}\hat{e}_3 ω=Ωe^z​+ψ˙​e^3​

Try breaking e^z\hat{e}_ze^z​ into its e^1\hat{e}_1e^1​ and e^3\hat{e}_3e^3​ components to rewrite ω⃗\vec{\omega}ω in terms of those unit vectors.

e^z=sin⁡θe^1+cos⁡θe^3 \hat{e}_z = \sin\theta\hat{e}_1 + \cos\theta\hat{e}_3 e^z​=sinθe^1​+cosθe^3​ ω⃗=Ωsin⁡θ+Ωcos⁡θe^3+ψ˙e^3 \vec{\omega} = \Omega\sin\theta + \Omega\cos\theta\hat{e}_3 + \dot{\psi}\hat{e}_3 ω=Ωsinθ+Ωcosθe^3​+ψ˙​e^3​

Now use the expression you got for ψ˙\dot{\psi}ψ˙​ above to get a final form for ω⃗\vec{\omega}ω

ω⃗=Ωsin⁡θ(e^1+ℓRe^3) \vec{\omega} = \Omega\sin\theta\left(\hat{e}_1 + \frac{\ell}{R}\hat{e}_3\right) ω=Ωsinθ(e^1​+Rℓ​e^3​)

It is not usually the case that the rotational velocity about two principal axes is the same, it leads to some interesting behavior for this system.

To get the angular momentum, should we multiply this angular velocity by a moment of inertia?

No. As in class, we have to recognize that there are multiple moments of inertia involved in this case so

L⃗=I1ω1e^1+I3ω3e^3 \vec{L} = I_1\omega_1\hat{e}_1 + I_3\omega_3\hat{e}_3 L=I1​ω1​e^1​+I3​ω3​e^3​

We found that ω1\omega_1ω1​ and ω2\omega_2ω2​ are the same so

L⃗=Ωsin⁡θ(I1e^1+ℓRI3e^3) \vec{L} = \Omega\sin\theta\left(I_1\hat{e}_1 + \frac{\ell}{R}I_3\hat{e}_3\right) L=Ωsinθ(I1​e^1​+Rℓ​I3​e^3​)

In terms of ω1\omega_1ω1​ and ω3\omega_3ω3​, what angle does ω⃗\vec{\omega}ω make with the e^3\hat{e}_3e^3​ vector? What is this in terms of ℓ\ellℓ and RRR? Similarly, what angle does L⃗\vec{L}L make with e^3\hat{e}_3e^3​? Are L⃗\vec{L}L and ω⃗\vec{\omega}ω in the same direction for this system?

Fig.2 - From Jackson et al., Am. J. Phys., Feb. 2019

γω=arctan⁡(ω1ω3)=arctan⁡(Rℓ) \gamma_\omega = \arctan\left(\frac{\omega_1}{\omega_3}\right) = \arctan\left(\frac{R}{\ell}\right) γω​=arctan(ω3​ω1​​)=arctan(ℓR​) γL=arctan⁡(L1L3)=arctan⁡(I1RI3ℓ) \gamma_L = \arctan\left(\frac{L_1}{L_3}\right) = \arctan\left(\frac{I_1R}{I_3\ell}\right) γL​=arctan(L3​L1​​)=arctan(I3​ℓI1​R​)

So they are not in the same direction.

L⃗\vec{L}L precesses as in the case of a tilted top or gyroscope, but its magnitude should not be significantly changed. In our case the angular momentum is given by

L⃗=I3Ωsin⁡θcos⁡θ[(I1I3−ℓRtan⁡θ)r^xy+(I1I3−ℓR)e^z] \vec{L} = I_3\Omega\sin\theta\cos\theta\left[\left(\frac{I_1}{I_3} - \frac{\ell}{R}\tan\theta\right)\hat{r}_{xy} + \left(\frac{I_1}{I_3} -\frac{\ell}{R}\right)\hat{e}_z\right] L=I3​Ωsinθcosθ[(I3​I1​​−Rℓ​tanθ)r^xy​+(I3​I1​​−Rℓ​)e^z​]

where r^xy\hat{r}_{xy}r^xy​ represents a unit vector in the x−yx-yx−y plane (it is rotatin in this plane at a rate Ω\OmegaΩ).

  2. Find an expression for the magnitude of L⃗\vec{L}L if (a) θ=γω\theta = \gamma_\omegaθ=γω​ and if (b) θ=γL\theta = \gamma_Lθ=γL​. Is there a significant difference between the two?

Experiment

  1. Measure the length and diameter of the hollow pvc cylinder.

  2. Set the cylinder spinning using one of the two methods shown in the videos below and record video that includes both the beginning of the spin and the beginning of the roll.

If you use different methods for different trials keep track of which method you used for which trial.

  1. Repeat at least three times using the same spinning method/technique.

  2. Analyze the video in tracker to find both the initial spin rate Ω\OmegaΩ of the cylinder and the linear velocity while rolling after it has finished spinning. Here is a useful guide on extracting rotational velocity in tracker.

  1. Repeat for the solid cylinder (wooden dowel).

Experimental tips:

  • I found that the twist method of starting the cylinder allows for more control of where the cylinder stays but it may be a bit difficult to get started compared to the push method. You may have to try repeatedly before you can get a useable video using any method. Don't give up!

  • I found it useful to define three different point masses in tracker:

One for the center of mass, which should be set as the reference frame as shown in the tutorial video and in the image below.

One for a point of the cylinder you track as it goes around the center of mass. This can be difficult. Tracking a point on the edge of the cylinder works well. Tracking a point marked with tape doesn't work as well as you might think – for interesting reasons – but give it a try.

One for the cylinder after it has stopped spinning to get the rolling motion separately.

In the end, you should have three tracks looking sort of like the tracks in the images below.

Autotracker probably only works for the rolling part of the motion.
